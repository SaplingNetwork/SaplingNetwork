# 2. Problems

## Non-renewable Energy and Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Despite their rapid growth, most cryptocurrencies remain environmentally unsustainable.

#### 1. Excessive Energy Consumption

Proof-of-Work–based networks consume enormous amounts of electricity to secure their blockchains. Bitcoin alone consumes over **200 TWh of electricity annually**, placing it among the world’s largest energy consumers. This energy demand is largely met through **fossil fuels**, significantly increasing global greenhouse gas emissions.

#### 2. High Carbon Emissions

Bitcoin mining produces over **200 tons of CO₂ per BTC**, making it up to **15 times more carbon-intensive than gold mining** for the same economic value. Collectively, cryptocurrencies emit **more CO₂ annually than many sovereign nations**, with Bitcoin accounting for approximately **65% of total crypto-related emissions**.

#### 3. Lack of Environmental Accountability

Most blockchain ecosystems externalize their environmental costs. Emissions generated by transactions, mining, and infrastructure are rarely offset, measured, or transparently reported. As a result, users and investors unknowingly contribute to climate degradation.

#### 4. Regulatory and Ethical Pressure

Governments have begun restricting mining activities due to disproportionate energy usage, as seen in countries such as China and Iran. Meanwhile, global initiatives like the **Crypto Climate Accord** demand a transition toward renewable energy and net-zero emissions — a transition many projects are unprepared to make.

Without fundamental innovation, cryptocurrency adoption and environmental sustainability remain fundamentally misaligned.
