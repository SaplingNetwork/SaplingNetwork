# 2. The Problem

#### 2.1 Concentrated, fossil-heavy energy consumption

Proof-of-Work networks consume enormous amounts of electricity. Bitcoin alone is estimated to draw **120–180 TWh annually** (Cambridge Bitcoin Electricity Consumption Index) — comparable to a mid-sized country — and a substantial share of this demand is still met by fossil fuels.

#### 2.2 High carbon emissions

Bitcoin accounts for the large majority of all crypto-related emissions. Even after Ethereum's transition to Proof of Stake in 2022 cut its energy use by **over 99.98%**, the industry's aggregate footprint remains in the tens of millions of tonnes of CO₂e per year — more than many sovereign nations.

#### 2.3 No environmental accountability

Most blockchain ecosystems externalize their environmental costs. Emissions generated by transactions, infrastructure, and mining are rarely offset, measured, or transparently reported. Users and investors contribute to climate degradation without knowing it — and without any tool to act on it.

#### 2.4 Growing regulatory and ethical pressure

Jurisdictions have restricted or banned mining over energy concerns, and frameworks such as the EU's MiCA now require crypto-asset service providers to disclose environmental impact. Initiatives like the **Crypto Climate Accord** push toward net-zero — a transition most projects are structurally unprepared for.

Without deliberate design, cryptocurrency adoption and environmental sustainability remain misaligned. Sapling Network is that deliberate design.
